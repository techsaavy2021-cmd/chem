C3.1 Formulas

1 State the formulas of the elements and compounds named in the subject content.

Learn and recall correct chemical formulas for all named substances in the syllabus (e.g. H2, O2, N2, H2O, CO2, NH3, CH4, NaCl, HCl, H2SO4, NaOH, etc.).

2 Define the molecular formula of a compound as the number and type of atoms in one molecule.

Molecular formula shows the actual number of each type of atom in one molecule, e.g. H2O has 2 H and 1 O; C2H6 has 2 C and 6 H.

3 Deduce the formula of a simple molecular compound from the relative numbers of atoms present in a model or a diagrammatic representation.

Count each atom in the model/diagram and write the formula, e.g. if there are 1 carbon and 4 hydrogens bonded → CH4.

4 Construct word equations to show how reactants form products.

Use names only, e.g. magnesium + hydrochloric acid → magnesium chloride + hydrogen.

5 Balance and interpret simple symbol equations, including state symbols.

Write formulas then adjust coefficients so each element has the same number of atoms on both sides, adding state symbols (s, l, g, aq).
Example: 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l).

6 Deduce the formula of an ionic compound from the relative numbers of the ions present in a model or a diagrammatic representation or from the charges on the ions.

From charges, use “cross‑over” so total charge is zero, e.g. Na⁺ and Cl⁻ → NaCl; Mg²⁺ and Cl⁻ → MgCl2. From a model, simplify the ratio of cations to anions.

7 Construct symbol equations with state symbols, including ionic equations.

Write full balanced equations with (s), (l), (g), (aq). For ionic equations, show only the species that change, e.g. Cu²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Cu(OH)2(s).

8 Deduce the symbol equation with state symbols for a chemical reaction, given relevant information.

From a description or word equation, decide on formulas, balance the equation and assign correct states using knowledge of soluble/insoluble substances and physical states.

C3.2 Relative masses of atoms and molecules

1 Describe relative atomic mass, Ar, as the average mass of the isotopes of an element compared to 1/12th of the mass of an atom of ¹²C.

Ar is a weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element, using the carbon‑12 scale where the mass of ¹²C is exactly 12.

2 Define relative molecular mass, Mr, as the sum of the relative atomic masses. Relative formula mass, Mr, will be used for ionic compounds.

Mr = sum of Ar of all atoms in the formula.
Examples: H2O: Mr = 2×1 + 16 = 18; NaCl: Mr = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5.

3 Calculate reacting masses in simple proportions (calculations will not involve the mole concept).

Use balanced equations to find simple mass ratios.
Example: 2H2 + O2 → 2H2O, if 2 g of H2 reacts completely, use relative masses to find mass of water formed.

C3.3 The mole and the Avogadro constant

1 State that concentration can be measured in g / dm³.

Concentration of a solution can be expressed as grams of solute per cubic decimetre of solution (g/dm³).

2 State that the mole, mol, is the unit of amount of substance and that one mole contains 6.02 × 10²³ particles; this number is the Avogadro constant.

One mole of any substance contains 6.02 × 10²³ particles (atoms, ions or molecules) – the Avogadro constant.

3 Use the relationship amount of substance (mol) = mass (g) / molar mass (g / mol) to calculate: (a) amount of substance (b) mass (c) molar mass (d) relative atomic mass or relative molecular / formula mass.

n = m / M where n is moles, m is mass, M is molar mass (same value as Mr in g/mol). Rearrange to find any unknown, e.g. m = nM.

4 Use the molar gas volume, taken as 24 dm³ at room temperature and pressure, r.t.p., in calculations involving gases.

At r.t.p., 1 mol of any gas occupies ≈ 24 dm³.
Moles of gas = volume (dm³) / 24; volume (dm³) = moles × 24.

5 Calculate stoichiometric reacting masses, limiting reactants, volumes of gases at r.t.p., including conversion between cm³ and dm³.

Use the balanced equation to find mole ratios, then:
– convert mass ↔ moles using n = m / M;
– convert moles ↔ gas volume using 24 dm³ per mol;
– identify a limiting reactant by comparing available moles with the required ratio;
– remember: 1 dm³ = 1000 cm³.
