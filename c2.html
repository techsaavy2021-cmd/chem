C2.1 Elements, compounds and mixtures

1 Describe the differences between elements, compounds and mixtures.

Element: pure substance with only one type of atom, cannot be chemically broken down.
Compound: two or more elements chemically bonded in a fixed ratio, with properties different from the original elements.
Mixture: two or more substances not chemically bonded; they keep their own properties and can be separated by physical methods.

C2.2 Atomic structure and the Periodic Table

1 Describe the structure of the atom as a central nucleus containing neutrons and protons, surrounded by electrons in shells.

The atom has a small dense nucleus containing protons and neutrons. Electrons move around the nucleus in shells (energy levels).

2 State the relative charges and relative masses of a proton, a neutron and an electron.

Proton: charge +1, relative mass 1.
Neutron: charge 0, relative mass 1.
Electron: charge −1, relative mass ≈0 (about 1/1836).

3 Define proton number / atomic number as the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.

Proton (atomic) number Z = number of protons in the nucleus; it identifies the element.

4 Define mass number / nucleon number as the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.

Mass (nucleon) number A = number of protons + number of neutrons.

5 Determine the electronic configuration of elements with proton number 1 to 20, e.g. 2,8,3.

Fill the shells in order: first shell holds 2 electrons, second and third hold up to 8. Examples:
H (1): 1
C (6): 2,4
Na (11): 2,8,1
Al (13): 2,8,3
Ca (20): 2,8,8,2.

6 State that: (a) Group VIII noble gases have a full outer shell (b) the number of outer-shell electrons is equal to the group number in Groups I to VII (c) the number of occupied electron shells is equal to the period number.

(a) Noble gases (Group VIII / 0) have a full outer shell → very unreactive.
(b) For Groups I–VII, group number = number of outer-shell electrons (e.g. Group II → 2 outer electrons).
(c) Period number = number of occupied shells, e.g. Period 3 elements have three shells.

C2.3 Isotopes

1 Define isotopes as different atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

Isotopes: atoms of the same element (same proton number) but different numbers of neutrons, so different mass numbers.

2 Interpret and use symbols for atoms, e.g. ¹²₆C, and ions, e.g. ³⁵₁₇Cl⁻.

In ¹²₆C: 6 = Z (protons), 12 = A (protons + neutrons), so neutrons = 12 − 6 = 6.
In ³⁵₁₇Cl⁻: protons = 17, neutrons = 35 − 17 = 18, electrons = 18 (one extra for the −1 charge).

3 State that isotopes of the same element have the same chemical properties because they have the same number of electrons and therefore the same electronic configuration.

Isotopes have the same electron arrangement, so they have the same chemical reactions; only some physical properties (e.g. mass, density, radioactivity) differ.

C2.4 Ions and ionic bonds

1 Describe the formation of positive ions, known as cations, and negative ions, known as anions.

Atoms can lose electrons to form positive ions (cations), usually metals, or gain electrons to form negative ions (anions), usually non-metals.

2 State that an ionic bond is a strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.

Ionic bond: strong attraction between positively and negatively charged ions.

3 Describe the formation of ionic bonds between elements from Group I and Group VII, including the use of dot-and-cross diagrams.

Group I metal atoms lose 1 electron to form M⁺, Group VII non-metal atoms gain 1 electron to form X⁻. Example: Na (2,8,1) loses 1 e⁻ → Na⁺ (2,8); Cl (2,8,7) gains 1 e⁻ → Cl⁻ (2,8,8). Na⁺ and Cl⁻ attract to form NaCl.

4 Describe the properties of ionic compounds: (a) high melting points and boiling points (b) good electrical conductivity when aqueous or molten and poor when solid (c) generally soluble in water.

(a) High m.p. and b.p. because many strong ionic bonds in the giant lattice must be broken.
(b) Conduct electricity when molten or in aqueous solution (ions are free to move), but not when solid (ions fixed in place).
(c) Many ionic compounds are soluble in water because water molecules help separate and surround the ions.

5 Describe the formation of ionic bonds between ions of metallic and non-metallic elements, including the use of dot-and-cross diagrams.

In general, a metal atom loses electrons to form a positive ion, and a non-metal atom gains electrons to form a negative ion. These oppositely charged ions attract and form a giant ionic lattice (e.g. Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ in MgO).

6 Explain in terms of structure and bonding the properties of ionic compounds: (a) high melting points and boiling points (b) good electrical conductivity when aqueous or molten and poor when solid.

The giant lattice has alternating positive and negative ions with strong electrostatic attractions; large amounts of energy are needed to break these bonds → high m.p./b.p.
In solids, ions cannot move, so they do not conduct; in molten/aqueous states, ions are mobile, so they conduct electricity.

7 Describe the giant lattice structure of ionic compounds as a regular arrangement of alternating positive and negative ions, exemplified by sodium chloride.

NaCl: a regular 3D structure where each Na⁺ is surrounded by Cl⁻ and each Cl⁻ is surrounded by Na⁺, forming a repeating pattern.

C2.5 Simple molecules and covalent bonds

1 State that a covalent bond is formed when a pair of electrons is shared between two atoms leading to noble gas electronic configurations.

Covalent bond: a shared pair of electrons; each atom achieves a full outer shell like a noble gas.

2 Describe the formation of covalent bonds in simple molecules, including H₂, Cl₂, H₂O, CH₄, NH₃ and HCl. Use dot-and-cross diagrams to show the electronic configurations in these molecules.

Each single bond is one shared pair. Examples: H₂ (H–H), Cl₂ (Cl–Cl), H₂O (O bonded to two H), CH₄ (C bonded to four H), NH₃ (N bonded to three H), HCl (H–Cl).

3 Describe in terms of structure and bonding the properties of simple molecular compounds: (a) low melting points and boiling points (b) poor electrical conductivity.

They consist of small molecules with strong covalent bonds inside each molecule but only weak forces between molecules, so they have low m.p./b.p. They have no free ions or electrons, so they do not conduct electricity.

4 Describe the formation of covalent bonds in simple molecules, including CH₃OH, C₂H₄, O₂, CO₂ and N₂. Use dot-and-cross diagrams to show the electronic configurations in these molecules.

Some have multiple bonds: O₂ has a double bond (O=O), N₂ has a triple bond (N≡N), C₂H₄ has a C=C double bond, CO₂ has two C=O double bonds, CH₃OH has C–H, C–O and O–H single bonds.

5 Explain in terms of structure and bonding the properties of simple molecular compounds: (a) low melting points and boiling points in terms of weak intermolecular forces (b) poor electrical conductivity.

Only weak intermolecular forces need to be overcome to melt or boil them → low m.p./b.p. Molecules are neutral and do not contain mobile charged particles → they are poor electrical conductors.

C2.6 Giant covalent structures

1 Describe the giant covalent structures of graphite and diamond.

Diamond: each carbon atom is bonded to four others in a 3D tetrahedral network.
Graphite: each carbon atom is bonded to three others in flat hexagonal layers; layers are held together by weak forces.

2 Relate the structures and bonding of graphite and diamond to their uses, limited to: (a) graphite as a lubricant and as an electrode (b) diamond in cutting tools.

Graphite: layers can slide over each other → used as a lubricant; each carbon has a delocalised electron → conducts electricity, so used as electrodes.
Diamond: all atoms held by strong covalent bonds → extremely hard, used in cutting tools and drills.

C2.7 Metallic bonding

1 Describe metallic bonding as the electrostatic attraction between the positive ions in a giant metallic lattice and a ‘sea’ of delocalised electrons.

Metal atoms lose outer-shell electrons to form positive ions in a lattice; their electrons become delocalised and move freely. The attraction between the positive ions and this electron sea is metallic bonding.

2 Explain in terms of structure and bonding the properties of metals: (a) good electrical conductivity (b) malleability.

(a) The delocalised electrons can move through the lattice and carry charge → metals are good electrical conductors.
(b) The layers of positive ions can slide past each other while staying bonded to the electron sea → metals are malleable and ductile.
