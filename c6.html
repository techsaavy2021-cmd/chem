C6.1 Physical and chemical changes

1 Identify physical and chemical changes, and understand the differences between them.

Physical change:
– No new substance is formed.
– Often easily reversible (by simple physical processes).
– Examples: changes of state (melting, boiling, freezing), dissolving salt in water, breaking glass, stretching a rubber band.

Chemical change:
– One or more new substances are formed.
– Usually difficult to reverse (needs another chemical reaction).
– Often involves colour change, gas production, temperature change, or a precipitate forming.
– Examples: burning magnesium, rusting iron, neutralisation, thermal decomposition.

C6.2 Rate of reaction

1 Describe the effect on the rate of reaction of: (a) changing the concentration of solutions (b) changing the pressure of gases (c) changing the surface area of solids (d) changing the temperature (e) adding or removing a catalyst.

(a) Concentration of solutions: higher concentration → particles are more crowded → more frequent collisions → faster rate.
(b) Pressure of gases: higher pressure → gas particles closer together → more collisions per second → faster rate.
(c) Surface area of solids: smaller pieces / powder → larger surface area → more particles exposed → more collisions → faster rate.
(d) Temperature: higher temperature → particles have more kinetic energy → more frequent and more energetic collisions → many more collisions with E ≥ Ea → much faster rate.
(e) Catalyst: presence of a catalyst provides an alternative route with lower activation energy → more effective collisions → faster rate. Removing catalyst slows reaction back to its uncatalysed rate.

2 State that a catalyst increases the rate of a reaction and is unchanged at the end of a reaction.

A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a reaction by providing an alternative pathway with lower activation energy. It is not used up in the reaction and is chemically unchanged overall at the end (can be recovered and reused).

3 Describe practical methods for investigating the rate of a reaction including change in mass of a reactant or product and the formation of a gas.

Common lab methods:
– Change in mass (for reactions producing a gas): place the reaction flask on a balance and record the mass at regular time intervals. As gas escapes, the mass decreases; steeper mass‑time curves mean faster reactions.
– Gas volume: collect gas in a gas syringe or upside‑down measuring cylinder and measure the volume at fixed time intervals; steeper volume‑time graphs mean faster reactions.
– Disappearing cross: for reactions that form a precipitate (e.g. sodium thiosulfate + acid), time how long it takes for a mark under the flask to become obscured; shorter time = faster rate.
– Colour change: use a colorimeter or estimate by eye over time.

4 Interpret data, including graphs, from rate of reaction experiments.

– On a mass‑time or volume‑time graph, the steeper the gradient, the faster the rate at that moment.
– Rate at a specific time = gradient of the tangent to the curve at that point.
– Reactions eventually level off when at least one reactant is used up (no further change in mass/volume).
– Comparing experiments: faster reactions reach the same final mass/volume in a shorter time.

5 Explain the effect on the rate of reaction of: (a) changing the concentration of solutions (b) changing the pressure of gases (c) changing the surface area of solids (d) changing the temperature (e) adding or removing a catalyst using collision theory.

Collision theory: for a reaction to occur, particles must collide with sufficient energy (≥ Ea) and with the correct orientation. Rate depends on the frequency of effective collisions.

(a) Higher concentration → more particles in the same volume → collisions occur more often → more effective collisions per second → rate increases.
(b) Higher gas pressure → particles closer together → more frequent collisions → higher rate.
(c) Greater surface area (e.g. powder vs lump) → more particles on the surface exposed → more frequent collisions between reactant particles → faster rate.
(d) Higher temperature → particles have more kinetic energy → move faster, collide more often and a larger proportion have energy ≥ Ea → very large increase in rate.
(e) Catalyst → lowers activation energy by providing an alternative reaction pathway → a larger fraction of collisions have sufficient energy to react → more effective collisions per second → increased rate.

6 State that a catalyst decreases the activation energy, Ea, of a reaction.

In terms of an energy profile diagram, a catalyst creates a new lower “hump” between reactants and products. This means the activation energy Ea is reduced, so more particles have enough energy to react at the same temperature.

7 Describe collision theory in terms of: (a) number of particles per unit volume (b) frequency of collisions between particles (c) kinetic energy of particles (d) activation energy, Ea.

– (a) Number of particles per unit volume: higher concentration/pressure gives more particles in a given volume, so more chances for collisions.
– (b) Frequency of collisions: the more often particles collide, the greater the chance of successful (effective) collisions per second, increasing rate.
– (c) Kinetic energy of particles: higher temperature increases particle speed and kinetic energy, affecting both collision frequency and the fraction of collisions that have enough energy.
– (d) Activation energy Ea: only collisions with energy ≥ Ea result in reaction. Lowering Ea (with a catalyst) increases the fraction of effective collisions, raising the rate.

C6.3 Redox

1 Define redox reactions as involving simultaneous oxidation and reduction.

A redox reaction is one in which reduction and oxidation occur at the same time. One substance is oxidised while another is reduced – they always happen together.

2 Define oxidation as gain of oxygen and reduction as loss of oxygen.

In terms of oxygen:
– Oxidation = gain of oxygen (e.g. Mg + O₂ → MgO, magnesium is oxidised).
– Reduction = loss of oxygen (e.g. CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O, copper(II) oxide is reduced to copper by losing oxygen).

3 Identify redox reactions as reactions involving gain and loss of oxygen.

If one substance gains oxygen while another loses oxygen in the same reaction, it is a redox reaction. Example: 2CuO + C → 2Cu + CO₂ – CuO loses oxygen (reduced to Cu).
– C gains oxygen (oxidised to CO₂). Both oxidation and reduction occur → redox.

4 Identify oxidation and reduction in redox reactions. (Oxidation number limited to its use to name ions, e.g. iron(II), iron(III), copper(II).)

– Look for gain of oxygen (oxidation) and loss of oxygen (reduction).
– In terms of electrons (for extended understanding): oxidation is loss of electrons, reduction is gain of electrons.
– Oxidation numbers are mainly used here for naming ions, e.g. iron(II) (Fe²⁺), iron(III) (Fe³⁺), copper(II) (Cu²⁺). A change in oxidation number (e.g. Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺) indicates oxidation; Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺ indicates reduction.

5 Define oxidation in terms of: (a) loss of electrons (b) an increase in oxidation number (determination of oxidation numbers is not required).

– (a) Oxidation is loss of electrons (remember OIL: Oxidation Is Loss).
– (b) Oxidation also corresponds to an increase in oxidation number (e.g. Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺).

6 Define reduction in terms of: (a) gain of electrons (b) a decrease in oxidation number (determination of oxidation numbers is not required).

– (a) Reduction is gain of electrons (remember RIG: Reduction Is Gain).
– (b) Reduction also corresponds to a decrease in oxidation number (e.g. Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺).
