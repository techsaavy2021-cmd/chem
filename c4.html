C4.1 Electrolysis

1 Define electrolysis as the decomposition of an ionic compound, when molten or in aqueous solution, by the passage of an electric current.

Electrolysis is the process in which an electric current causes a chemical decomposition of an ionic compound that is either molten or dissolved in water (aqueous). The compound splits into simpler substances (elements or simpler ions).

2 Identify in simple electrolytic cells: (a) the anode as the positive electrode (b) the cathode as the negative electrode (c) the electrolyte as the molten or aqueous substance that undergoes electrolysis.

(a) Anode: the positive electrode, connected to the positive terminal of the power supply.
(b) Cathode: the negative electrode, connected to the negative terminal of the power supply.
(c) Electrolyte: the molten or aqueous ionic compound that contains the mobile ions and is decomposed during electrolysis.

3 Identify the products formed at the electrodes and describe the observations made during the electrolysis of: (a) molten lead(II) bromide (b) concentrated aqueous sodium chloride (c) dilute sulfuric acid using inert electrodes made of platinum or carbon / graphite.

(a) Molten lead(II) bromide, PbBr₂ (inert electrodes):
– Cathode (−): Pb²⁺(l) + 2e⁻ → Pb(l). You see silvery molten lead forming at the bottom.
– Anode (+): 2Br⁻(l) → Br₂(g) + 2e⁻. Brown/orange bromine vapour appears at the anode.

(b) Concentrated aqueous sodium chloride (brine) with inert electrodes:
– Cathode: mainly water is reduced: 2H₂O(l) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g) + 2OH⁻(aq). You see bubbles of hydrogen gas.
– Anode: chloride ions are oxidised: 2Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻. Greenish chlorine gas with a sharp smell is produced.
– The remaining solution contains Na⁺ and OH⁻ → it becomes aqueous sodium hydroxide (alkaline).

(c) Dilute sulfuric acid, H₂SO₄(aq) with inert electrodes:
– Cathode: 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g). Bubbles of hydrogen gas at the cathode.
– Anode: 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻. Bubbles of oxygen gas at the anode.
– Overall, water is being electrolysed into hydrogen and oxygen.

4 Describe the transfer of charge during electrolysis: (a) the movement of electrons in the external circuit (b) the loss or gain of electrons at the electrodes (c) the movement of ions in the electrolyte.

(a) In the external circuit, electrons flow from the negative terminal of the power supply to the cathode, and from the anode back to the positive terminal.
(b) At the cathode (−), ions gain electrons from the electrode (this is reduction). At the anode (+), ions or atoms lose electrons to the electrode (this is oxidation).
(c) In the electrolyte: positive ions (cations) move towards the cathode to be reduced; negative ions (anions) move towards the anode to be oxidised. This ion movement completes the circuit inside the solution.

5 Identify the products formed at the electrodes and describe the observations made during the electrolysis of aqueous copper(II) sulfate using carbon / graphite electrodes and when using copper electrodes.

With carbon/graphite (inert) electrodes:
– Cathode: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s). Pink/brown solid copper is deposited on the cathode surface.
– Anode: 4OH⁻(aq) → O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻. Bubbles of oxygen gas form at the anode.
– The blue colour of the CuSO₄ solution fades as Cu²⁺ ions are removed from solution.

With copper electrodes (copper anode and copper cathode):
– Cathode (pure copper): Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s). Copper is deposited on the cathode, so its mass increases.
– Anode (impure copper): Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻. Copper dissolves into solution, so the anode loses mass and impurities fall off.
– The blue colour of the CuSO₄ solution stays almost the same because Cu²⁺ ions leaving the anode are replaced by those arriving at the cathode. This is used in purification of copper.

6 State that metals or hydrogen are formed at the cathode and that non-metals (other than hydrogen) are formed at the anode.

In general:
– At the cathode (−): metals or hydrogen gas are produced.
– At the anode (+): non‑metals (other than hydrogen) such as halogens (Cl₂, Br₂, I₂) or oxygen (O₂) are produced.

7 Predict the identity of the products at each electrode for the electrolysis of a binary compound in the molten state.

For a molten binary ionic compound (only two elements present), e.g. NaCl, MgBr₂:
– Cathode: the metal cation is reduced to the metal, e.g. Na⁺ → Na, Mg²⁺ → Mg.
– Anode: the non‑metal anion is oxidised to the non‑metal element, e.g. 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂, 2Br⁻ → Br₂.
There is no water or other ions to interfere, so prediction is straightforward.

8 Construct ionic half-equations for reactions at the cathode (showing gain of electrons as a reduction reaction).

At the cathode, write equations showing ions gaining electrons. Examples:
– Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)
– Pb²⁺(l) + 2e⁻ → Pb(l)
– 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H₂(g)
These are all reduction half‑equations.

C4.2 Hydrogen–oxygen fuel cells

1 State that a hydrogen–oxygen fuel cell uses hydrogen and oxygen to produce electricity with water as the only chemical product.

A hydrogen–oxygen fuel cell is a device that converts the chemical energy of hydrogen and oxygen directly into electrical energy. The overall reaction in the cell is:
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l) The only chemical product is water (no carbon‑containing gases from the cell itself).

2 Describe the advantages and disadvantages of using hydrogen–oxygen fuel cells in comparison with gasoline / petrol engines in vehicles.

Advantages compared with petrol engines:
– The only emission at the point of use is water → no CO₂, CO or NOₓ from the cell, so it can help reduce air pollution and greenhouse gas emissions (depending on hydrogen source).
– Fuel cells can be more efficient at converting chemical energy to electrical energy than combustion engines converting chemical energy to heat and then mechanical work.
– They can provide quiet and smooth operation with fewer moving parts than an engine.

Disadvantages:
– Hydrogen storage and transport are difficult: hydrogen is a very low‑density, flammable gas that must be stored under high pressure or as a liquid, which needs special tanks and safety systems.
– At the moment, much hydrogen is produced from fossil fuels (e.g. by steam reforming of natural gas), which can still generate CO₂ overall.
– Fuel cells and hydrogen infrastructure (production, refuelling stations) are currently expensive and not as widely available as petrol and diesel stations.
– Refuelling and production require new technology and investment compared to existing petrol engine systems.
